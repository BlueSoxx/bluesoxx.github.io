General Questions

When/Where will the class meet?

The May/June introductory classes will take place at Suite Genius Mount Pleasant, near the Broadway-City Hall Skytrain station.

The classes will take place after school, once or twice a week.  Exact dates will be determined after May 15, so we can accomodate the schedules of registered students.  Then, we will take it from there for when and where to hold the summer program!

Who will teach the class?

Eric Hsu will lead the first classes, and more instructors will come on later according to demand.  “My academic background is in artificial intelligence, but a few years ago I went back into industry, building software systems for startups in San Francisco and Boston.  I’ve taught programming for software engineers, and computer science at university.  I’ve also taught leadership at military school, P.E. for first-graders, and will start coaching beginners at Vancouver Rowing Club this summer.  I moved to Vancouver one year ago with my wife, who took a job at UBC, and started working remotely for a U.S. company while waiting for Canadian residency.  Now I have a work permit, but miss teaching!  Also I love the general attitude of the newest generation of people and am jealous of all the opportunities and resources they have now for going through life.”

The program will also use online computer science courses made available by universities with track records of successful undergraduate education.  Most importantly, though, students will work together to explore the material and pursue their own understanding as a cohort.

How is a computer science class different from a “learn to code” camp?   What is the difference between coding and computer science?

Students will learn both coding, and computer science as a whole: a lot of coders know some computer science and wish they knew more; most computer scientists can code, but they would certainly like to code better.  Coding uses existing and emerging programming languages to create apps or backend software or user interfaces that people can use on their computing platform.  Computer science figures out new things that can/can’t be computed (not everything can be computed!) and the techniques for doing so in the best (fastest, most reliable, cheapest) way possible.

An analogy is that a lot of people can drive a car, just as a lot of people can use a computer.  But, a master mechanic can not only operate, or repair, or customize a car, but can even make a new one from mixed parts, just as coders can extend existing software applications, or create entirely new ones.  A computer scientist is more like an automotive engineer, in terms of designing new kinds of vehicles, understanding what makes them faster (or safer, or easier to build), and creating new parts that mechanics can use.  So figuring out how to make a personal assistant like Siri understand what you are saying is more about computer science, and building Siri is more about coding.

A final distinction is that some shorter-term coding camps are more about quickly getting a small demo project working using pre-built templates or beginners' platforms (like the most excellent Scratch language.)  This could be compared to assembling a model car from a kit: it is a practical and achievable short-term goal, and a great way to get familiar with cars (or computers) and learn something about how they work.  But over a longer course of time the Bluesoxx program will go beyond simple projects so participants can create realistic programs built with the same tools, methods, and theoretical foundations used by professionals.

What is the minimum experience level for the course?

No experience is required to take the course.

How will this girls-only program differ from a co-ed one?

Most everyone who has worked with women, or studied women, or met a woman, or who is a woman, has some sort of understanding about how women are different from men and how women can be successful in different ways.  But, the Bluesoxx program has no doctrine about the best way to teach girls, and no policy regarding their relationship with technology and society, except that the course is currently open only to people who identify as female and attend a Vancouver-area secondary school.

Student Questions

Will it be fun?

Yes!  After a few lessons you will be able make some basic things like a professional-style (i.e. not from a template) web page, or a word-guessing game like “Hangman”, or a never-ending drawing like the Sierpiński Triangle.

But, you will have to work, too: we won’t be doing a kid’s version of programming where you can quickly make something from a kit to show your parents but nobody wants to explain how it works.  It is kind of like taking piano lessons: it’s not fun just start playing on a piano when you don’t know how to play.  But, it is fun to work out how to do something that you can't do, and then do it!  Getting good at something over time can more fun than something that is just entertaining.  And, you will be doing it with your friends.

The opportunity to work with your friends and accomplish things together is one advantage over the specific example of playing the piano. Also, once you get started there are more resources to get really good on your own, without always having to meet up with a teacher to progress. (Playing the piano is surely a lot of fun for many people, too!)

Why might I be motivated to learn computer science?

If you already know of something that you enjoy learning more than computer science, then you should go ahead and do that for sure!

If, most likely, you are unsure how much you enjoy C.S., then come out and see if you like it!  One way describing it is that programming is like building something the way you want using facts and logic instead of persuasion or authority (like when you are debating something with your friends, versus with people who are younger or older than you), and that computer science is programming plus solving puzzles by figuring out the "trick" to making them work (like when you have your own favorite way of arranging your candies in Candy Crush.)

If it turns out that computer science is for you, then luckily you will be able to do something you like while transforming almost every part of the world, not just the computer industry.  Many of the newest and most important trends in a variety of areas require an understanding of computer science:

  • If you want to work in arts or entertainment, you can create digital art and animation using programming, or use algorithms to predict what kinds of movies people want to watch.
  • If you want to work in biology, or medicine, you can use computation to create new kinds of devices and programs to study people’s DNA and find a treatment for their condition.
  • If you want to work in literature you can use books to understand the purpose of life by scanning them all into a database and then analyzing them with a computer.
  • If you like nature you can discourage humans from destroying it by trying to convince them that they are doing so.
  • If you like sports you can use sensors and computers to figure out how your team can train better or play better and win more games.
  • If you want to do something that nobody has ever done before, there is a good chance that you will have to use a computer.
  • If you end up working primarily in a field that is not directly related to computation, it is still pretty straightforward to write some apps in your spare time.
  • If you want to travel the world telling young women how to empower themselves and improve their lives by taking charge, maybe you don’t have to study computer science…maybe go into management (and work really hard!)
  • If you don’t like to work you can program for fun, or go to grad school in computer science and do whatever you feel like (only partly joking.)

Also, you can make good income working with computers in the future, and live a fun life in the city you want, (or while travelling around the world), at least compared to being dependent on other people for money—you could also make mega-money by inventing a company or technology.  (If you already know that you don’t like C.S., but you need a solid salary, like, to provide for your family, then you should also learn C.S. but that is a pretty grim scenario indeed for a high school student.)

What can I expect to accomplish from these courses?

After a couple of classes, you will start to understand how computers work, and be able to write some very simple programs.  If you choose to stay on for a something like 12 weeks, you will have the same computer science ability as a computer science major doing their first year at a university like Harvey Mudd, MIT, or Stanford (because we will be using their actual courses.)

What if I can already program?

If you can already write programs, you will get to learn about the computer science algorithms and technologies that programming is based on, which will multiply your ability to do new things.  In terms of programming tasks, you will sometimes get a more challenging version of what everyone else is working on—the rest of the time you will get to carry out a time-honored and equally beneficial tradition in the history of computers: sharing what you’ve learned with others.

Is there really physical exercise?

Yah, one of the drawbacks of working with computers is that in the long term it can take a toll on your health and fitness.  So, we will maintain a habit taking fifteen minutes per class for something like yoga or the seven-minute workout, or some other exercise that you will lead.

Parent / Guardian Questions

Why should I be motivated for my offspring to learn computer science?

Learning C.S. might be really fun and interesting for your child, and also provide a means for them to pursue the career they want in a variety of fields in the future (a few examples are listed above in the Students' section).   Most children are aware early on as to whether they enjoy tumbling, dancing, fashion, or fighting, without taking gymnastics, ballet, sewing, or mixed-martial-arts classes first; but it may not be immediately obvious whether they will enjoy a less traditional activity, like computer science.  So, it is worth at least trying out!

Will a class like this help / hinder my child with getting into university?

Though the program is structured as a class, the best way to think of it as an extra-curricular activity like music lessons, sports, volunteering, or working a summer job.  So, if your child does not enjoy the program, making them take it will hinder them in life.

If it turns out that they do like computer science, it is definitely an under-appreciated means of enriching the standard offerings at most secondary schools, and in turn improving your child's chances for getting into a famous school.  Some parents are really concerned about marks and taking time away from study…but for the schools with tightest admissions rates, most applicants have pretty good marks already—a better differentiator might be working on something like a science project, a personal hobby, a non-profit organization, or a global open-source project, potentially using computer science ability.

Why shouldn't my child just take some coding classes online?

There are some excellent educational resources available online, mostly at no cost; we can definitely recommend some appropriate options for anyone who does not want to join in the course!  Indeed, the Bluesoxx program is built on many of these resources, so a more direct question might be, "Why shouldn’t my child just take some classes online by herself?"

Anyone who has taken an online computer science class—or built pretty much anything on a computer on their own, has probably shared the same experience with millions of beginning and expert users around the world: 90% of your project goes smoothly, the way it is supposed to; and 10% totally doesn’t make sense and is almost impossible to solve without help.   (Often, that 10% is the first 10% of the project!)  The opportunity to consult with fellow students, along with expert instructors, is key to cruising past obstacles that can otherwise dominate the entire learning experience.

Furthermore, technical competence in and of itself is not professionally or personally profitable.  Telling your child to just learn computer science online is comparable, on a smaller scale, to telling them to just go to university online.

Professionally, simply knowing how to do a task is not the same as knowing how to communicate with peers and stakeholders, how to identity new opportunities and problems that need solving, how to clarify requirements and manage expectations, or knowing how to ask the right question to get the right information at the right time.  (This may explain why so many technically proficient individuals are repeatedly cheated out of professional opportunities—by a combination of wrongfully and rightfully assessed factors of this kind.)

Personally, it can be ungratifying indeed to excel at something without the opportunity to teach and to be taught, to build inter-personal relationships, and to at least share your trials and accomplishments with some of part the wider world.  Not everybody in the world can be friends, but at the same time human beings do not seem to have evolved to perform solitary labor.  And as much as modern commerce is driven by mass production, and as much as this is reflected in the economic drive to extend automation into education, human mentorship and collaborative learning cannot currently be replaced by IT.

By these arguments, your child can definitely learn almost anything online on their own, including computer science; but it will take a lot longer and they will be missing out on many opportunities for personal enrichment.

What materials will my student need?

Please provide your student with a recent-model laptop...a Macbook will be more convenient but is not a necessity.
