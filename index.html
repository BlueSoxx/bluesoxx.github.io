Spring 2017: Programming with Python

Computer Science Program for High School Girls

Find Out More

Learn to program using real-world methods!

Bluesoxx hosts computer science programs for high school girls in Vancouver, BC.

This spring we will be improving our ability with the Python programming language, while emphasizing our general understanding of algorithms for getting the computer to accomplish our tasks efficiently.   Along the way we will use professional-style tools like Git (to collaborate on multiple versions of our programs) and Linux (for running our programs.)  

Bluesoxx will meet weekly on Thursdays from 4:00 to 6:30pm on a drop-in basis, starting March 30.   Our meeting site will be Istuary Innovation Group headquarters, located at 1125 Howe Street / 8th Floor, near Yaletown in Downtown Vancouver.   The spring session will run for ten weeks and carry a program fee of $60 to cover costs.  (Please contact info@bluesoxx.com in case of financial hardship.)

We won't always choose the most beginner-friendly path for writing Python code, because we want to emphasize real-world methods—but we are a friendly (and beginner-friendly) group and we learn together!  No programming experience of any kind is required to join with Bluesoxx.

More About Bluesoxx

About Bluesoxx

First Class Ambitions

We will definitely make some fun and simple demo projects.   But overall, the class is about real fundamentals in formal computer science, and professional-level programming.   We will also work on leadership, presentation skills, and health and fitness: less crafting-for-kids and more being-a-boss.

Getting it Done Together

The Bluesoxx program combines online materials from university-level computer science courses with personalized instruction by a local expert.   Most importantly, though, students work together to build and share expertise as a team, and take the lead in making this happen: not quite like joining a sorority, but more than just taking a class.

Real Accomplishments

The ability to use software, like the ability to read 300 years ago, is transforming assumptions about what people can accomplish.   Early birds who progress from computer-literate to best-selling-authors of computation are not just ensuring the income or lifestyle they want—they have the opportunity to impact the world.
